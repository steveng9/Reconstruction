As for what those lines mean — they're a quick sanity check printed before the AUC is computed. Each attack assigns every target record a    
  membership score (higher = more likely a training member). The print shows:                                                                  
                                                                                                                                               
  - SynthDistance — mean -dist(target, synth) for members vs non-members. If members are closer to the synthetic data (as expected when the    
  model memorised them), the member mean should be less negative (closer to 0).                                                                
  - NNDR — mean dist(target, train_LOO) / dist(target, synth) for each group. A higher ratio means "relatively closer to synth than to training
   distribution" → more likely a member.
  - RA-as-MIA — mean rarity-weighted reconstruction accuracy for members vs non-members. If the synthetic data reconstructs training members'  
  hidden features better than holdout records, the member mean should be higher.                                                               
                                                                                                                                               
  The gap between the two numbers is the raw signal the attack is exploiting. A negligible gap (as you're seeing with NNDR ~0.93 vs ~0.91) is
  consistent with low AUC — the attack isn't finding much.





The threshold is the median of the full 20k set (train + holdout combined), not just the training records. So it's the global 50th percentile
   of all scores.
                                                                                                                                               
  The four quadrants are then computed on the 10k training records only. They always sum to exactly n_train (10k here): 1042 + 4036 + 1211 +
  3711 = 10,000. ✓

  - Both-high (1042): training records where both MIA score ≥ global median AND RA score ≥ global median
  - MIA-only (4036): training records where MIA score ≥ median but RA score < median
  - RA-only (1211): training records where RA score ≥ median but MIA score < median
  - Both-low (3711): training records where both scores < median

  And yes, both-high ≤ min(both-high + MIA-only, both-high + RA-only) by definition — it's the intersection.

  On TPR: not quite. The quadrant counts are at the median threshold, and since the median splits all 20k scores in half, exactly 10k records
  (train + holdout combined) are above it. If the attack were perfect, all 10k training records would be above the median — so both-high +
  MIA-only = 10k and both-low + RA-only = 0. With no signal at all, you'd expect ~5k training records above the median by chance, split
  randomly between both-high and MIA-only. Here MIA-only=4036 + both-high=1042 = 5,078 ≈ 5k, which tells you MIA has almost no signal for this
  SDG method.

  Spearman r and p: the Spearman correlation measures how monotonically related the MIA scores and RA-as-MIA scores are, computed only on the
  training records. r=0.093 means almost no correlation — the two methods are picking up largely different (and both weak) signals. The
  p=0.0000 just means this tiny correlation is statistically distinguishable from zero, which is easy at n=10k even for noise-level effects. A
  large r (say >0.5) would mean "when MIA thinks a record is a member, RA tends to agree" — useful for understanding whether the two signals
  are redundant or complementary.

  anti-correlated (records ranked in opposite order). You're right that negative is possible in principle — if RA tends to score high exactly

  Your second point is exactly right. Two random noise signals can be highly correlated with each other while both being uncorrelated with     
  ground truth membership. Spearman r only measures agreement between the two signals, not agreement with truth.                               

  Your converse question is the interesting one. If both attacks are very accurate, what's the minimum possible correlation between them?

  Intuitively: if both have high TPR at the median threshold, then both-high + MIA-only ≈ 10k and both-high + RA-only ≈ 10k. Since all four
  quadrants sum to 10k, that forces both-high to be large and both-low to be small, which pushes r upward. So two highly-accurate attacks
  cannot be highly anti-correlated — they're geometrically forced to agree on many records.

  More precisely: if TPR_MIA = a and TPR_RA = b (both at median threshold, so FPR ≈ 0.5 for a random attack), then both-high ≥ a + b - 1 (by
  inclusion-exclusion). So if both have TPR=0.9, both-high ≥ 0.8 × n_train — they must agree on at least 80% of training records. High accuracy
   implies a lower bound on correlation, though the exact Spearman r depends on the full ranking not just the median split.

  So the relationship is asymmetric: high correlation tells you nothing about accuracy, but high accuracy implies a correlation floor.
