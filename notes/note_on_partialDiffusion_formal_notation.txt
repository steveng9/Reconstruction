What the model is learning                                                                                                                                    
                                                                                                                                                                
  The TabDDPM is trained on the synthetic data, learning the joint distribution P(QI, hidden). At inference time it conditions on the target's known QI values  
  to impute the hidden ones. The memorization signal emerges naturally: if the SDG memorized a training record (QI=q, hidden=h), there will be many synthetic   
  samples near (q, h), so the diffusion model learns P(hidden=h | QI=q) is high — and predicts h when given q.
                                                                                                                                                                
  So "good training" here means accurately modeling the synthetic data's joint distribution, not detecting overfitting per se. The overfitting in the SDG is    
  already baked into the synth data; you just need the TabDDPM to capture it faithfully.                                                                        
                                                                                                                                                                
  What actually matters, in rough order of impact:                                                                                                              

  1. resamples — the single most impactful knob
    - For TabDDPM: draws N independent reconstructions and picks the best-fitting one. More = better quality at cost of inference time. Try 20–50.
    - For RePaint: controls refinement passes (back-and-forth in the diffusion process). RePaint is much more sensitive to this — try 20–50 and you may see
  meaningfully better results.
  2. jump_fn (RePaint only) — controls how far back each refinement pass jumps. jump_max10 means ≤10 timesteps per jump. Larger jumps cover more of the
  diffusion trajectory but take longer. You might try a custom function that jumps further (e.g. 20–30).
  3. Training convergence — your loss at step 100k was GLoss: 0.034. The key question is whether it was still declining at that point. If it plateaued well
  before 100k, extra steps don't help. Your cosine scheduler already decays the LR automatically, so there's no reason to manually lower it.
  4. num_timesteps — 2000 is overkill for a 15-feature dataset. 1000 (the original default) is sufficient and trains faster. More timesteps make the diffusion
  process finer-grained but don't add signal beyond a point.
  5. Architecture — [512, 1024, 1024, 1024, 512] is already larger than the dataset complexity warrants. This is fine but not a bottleneck.

  The fundamental ceiling is the synthetic data quality: no amount of TabDDPM tuning can extract a memorization signal that isn't there. If the SDG didn't
  memorize much (e.g. high-ε DP methods), RA_delta will be near zero regardless. The random baseline score you're now printing will help you see this — if
  TabDDPM barely beats random on a non-memorizing SDG, that's expected.

  Practical starting point: Leave training as-is, and iterate on resamples (try 20, 50) and jump_fn for RePaint. Those are fast to tune since they don't require
   retraining.
