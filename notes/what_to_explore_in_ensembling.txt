This is a really common finding and worth thinking through carefully. Here's my honest take:                                  
                                                                                                                                
  Why 2-attack ensembles aren't helping                                                                                         
                                                                                                                                
  The core requirement for ensembling to help is error complementarity — attack A must be right on records where B is wrong, and
   vice versa. Your 8 attacks are actually quite correlated in their error patterns: RF, LGB, MLP, and LR are all discriminative
   classifiers trained on the same QI features, so they tend to fail on the same hard records (rare/outlier values where the
  QI→hidden relationship is weak). Voting just reinforces the shared mistakes.

  What I'd explore, roughly in order of promise:

  1. Oracle analysis first (diagnostic, not a paper experiment)
  Before trying anything, compute the per-record "oracle ensemble" — for each target row, take the correct answer if any attack
  got it right. If the oracle is only marginally better than the best single attack, there's genuinely little complementarity
  and ensembling is a dead end. If it's substantially better, the potential exists and you just need smarter aggregation.

  2. Feature-level attack selection
  Rather than a single attack for all hidden features, use different attacks per feature — e.g., whichever attack historically
  scores highest on that feature type across the synth samples. This isn't really an ensemble in the voting sense, but it's a
  natural composition that often beats any single attack.

  3. Include fundamentally different attack types
  Your 8 attacks are architecturally similar. TabDDPM/ConditionedRePaint are generative models that reason about the full joint
  distribution rather than feature-by-feature classification — they make structurally different errors. Even if slow, including
  one diffusion attack in a 2-attack ensemble is the most likely combination to find genuine complementarity.

  4. Weighted soft voting by per-feature accuracy
  Equal weights assume all attacks are equally good at all features, which isn't true. If you weight each attack's probability
  contribution by its historical accuracy on that specific feature (estimated from synth), the ensemble can learn to trust the
  right expert per feature. This is essentially a lightweight stacking approach without a meta-classifier.

  5. Agreement-based selection
  When attacks agree, use the agreed prediction. When they disagree, fall back to the single strongest attack (rather than
  voting, which can produce the minority answer). This is conservative but safe — it can't hurt relative to the best single
  attack.

  6. Larger ensembles of diverse attacks
  Going to 3-4 attacks only helps if the added attacks are diverse. Adding a 3rd tree-based classifier to RF+LGB won't move the
  needle. A more productive 3-attack ensemble might be something like RF + PartialMST + ConditionedRePaint — one discriminative,
   one graphical model, one generative.

  My honest overall take: if the best single attack is already well-calibrated and your QI→hidden signal is genuinely weak for
  hard records, no ensemble will beat it on mean RA. The more interesting paper result might actually be why ensembling doesn't
  help — it speaks to the nature of the residual uncertainty after conditioning on QI. The oracle analysis would make that
  argument precisely.
