UPDATE ON OUTLINE:

● Critical Assessment                              

  What Works Well                                                                                                                                                               
   
  - Jumping to Section 2 immediately is the right call. No reader wants four pages of setup before seeing anything happen.                                                      
  - Section 5's structure (memorization → MIA → disparate impact) is the strongest in the outline. These three build on each other naturally: "do attacks work?" → "what do they
   mean for privacy?" → "who is most at risk?"
  - NIST CRC as anchor in Section 3 is a great device — it gives the reader a concrete, real-world benchmark to orient around before you discuss modifications.
  - Appendix organization is clean. Pushing SDG method descriptions there is correct — they'd kill the main paper's pace.

  ---
  What Won't Work

  1. The intro subsections read like a prelims section in disguise.
  Subsections 1.1–1.3 with formal definitions will feel like a textbook. The intro should tell a story and make the reader care. Definitions can be woven in inline (one
  sentence each), or deferred. DP in particular — do you need the full ε definition in 1.2, or just "DP is a mathematical privacy guarantee parameterized by ε; smaller ε =
  stronger privacy"? The reader who doesn't know DP won't be helped by a one-paragraph subsection, and the reader who does doesn't need it.

  2. Section 2 promises results but delivers a survey.
  You said you'd distribute experiments 2–5, but Section 2 as written is pure taxonomy — descriptions of attacks with no numbers. The reader asks: which attack wins? You don't
  answer that until Section 4 at the earliest. This is the most serious flow problem.

  3. Section 3 has too many sub-experiments, especially ensembling.
  Four ensemble-finding methods × four visualization types = 16 mini-experiments stuffed into one subsection. The reader will get lost. What's the one insight ensembling
  delivers? Build around that.

  4. 2.5 and 2.6 are sidebars that interrupt the taxonomy.
  "Note on similarity to healthcare imputation" and "Acknowledging other similar RAs" don't belong in the taxonomy section. 2.6 is related work in disguise. Move it to the
  intro or a footnote. 2.5 is a one-line observation — make it a footnote or drop it.

  5. Section 4's organizing principle is just "experimental variables."
  Data type / data size / QI = three axes of a grid search. This will read like an experimental appendix. There's no argument being made. What's the insight each subsection
  delivers? You need a claim (e.g., "DP's protective effect collapses above ε = 1") not just a variable (e.g., "4.2 data size").

  6. No "main result moment."
  A reader finishing Section 5 should feel they've been on a journey. But right now there's no clear climactic result — no table or figure the reader can point to and say "this
   is the paper's core finding." The structure needs a spine.

  ---
  Three Alternative Outlines

  ---
  Option A: "Rising Stakes" (recommended)

  The logic: start with a simple question, complicate it progressively, and arrive at policy implications with the reader fully equipped.

  1. Introduction
     - Hook: what can an adversary learn from synthetic data alone?
     - RA definition, R_adv (inline, 2 sentences each)
     - DP definition (1 paragraph, motivated by: "this is the guarantee we're stress-testing")
     - Paper roadmap

  2. A Taxonomy of Reconstruction Attacks
     - 2.1 Baselines (Mode, Random, Mean)
     - 2.2 ML classifiers (Stadler et al.)
     - 2.3 Novel: Partially-instantiated SDGs (PartialMST, TabDDPM variants)
     - 2.4 Linear reconstruction (state-of-the-art, LP/Gurobi)
     [EXPERIMENT: one main table — all attacks × all SDG methods, single dataset, QI1]
     [KEY INSIGHT: simple baselines are deceptively competitive / diffusion is best / etc.]

  3. How Does the Scenario Shape Risk?  [SWAPPED from ch.4 — results here are stronger/cleaner than enhancements]
     - 3.1 SDG method and DP epsilon
       [EXPERIMENT: RA vs. ε curves — the key privacy-utility question]
       [+ data quality sidebar: does better synthetic data = higher attack success?]
     - 3.2 Dataset characteristics (type, size, column count, cardinality)
       [EXPERIMENT: 2–3 datasets showing qualitatively different regimes;
        per-feature RA breakdown; LP extended experiments (multi-valued + joint)]
     - 3.3 The choice of quasi-identifiers
       [EXPERIMENT: QI size vs. RA curve (adult QI_tiny/QI1/QI_large);
        composition contrast (QI1 vs. QI_behavioral, matched size)]

  4. Making Attacks Stronger  [SWAPPED from ch.3 — more speculative, can reference ch.3 datasets/findings]
     - 4.1 Chaining: sequential feature prediction
       [EXPERIMENT: chain order matters; show one figure of per-feature accuracy with/without chaining]
     - 4.2 Ensembling: combining attack predictions
       [EXPERIMENT: one figure — accuracy vs. ensemble size; one heatmap of pairwise attack correlations]
     - 4.3 NIST CRC: a real-world stress test
       [where auxiliary data enhancement changes the threat model — frame why it's out-of-scope for auditing]

  5. What Do Attacks Actually Reveal?
     - 5.1 Memorization vs. inference
       [EXPERIMENT: memorization test — RA_train vs. RA_nontraining delta]
     - 5.2 Relationship to membership inference
       [EXPERIMENT: RA-as-MIA vs. dedicated MIA, on same models]
     - 5.3 Disparate impact: who is most at risk?
       [EXPERIMENT: RA by subgroup / feature rarity — ties back to R_adv weighting]

  6. Implications for Privacy Auditing and Policy

  Appendix: prelims, hyperparams, implementation details, additional results

  Why this works: each section answers one question, experiments are one or two per section, and there's a clear spine: what attacks exist → how strong can we make them → what
  determines risk → what do results mean.

  ---
  Option B: "Question-Driven"

  Replace section titles with research questions. More provocative, better for engagement.

  2. Which Attacks Work? (taxonomy + main results table)
  3. Can We Do Better? (modifications + NIST CRC)
  4. Under What Conditions? (scenario variation)
  5. What Does "Success" Actually Mean? (memorization, MIA, disparate impact)
  6. Should We Worry? (policy + discussion)

  Same content as Option A, but framed as questions throughout. Works well if your venue allows it (workshop papers, PETS, FAccT). Riskier for traditional ML venues.

  ---
  Option C: Lead with the Punchline

  Open Section 2 with your most striking result — e.g., "a simple majority-vote baseline achieves X% of the best attack's performance" or "DP at ε=1 reduces attack success by
  only Y%" — then spend Sections 3–5 explaining why. This is a "conclusion first" structure that works well for readers who are skeptical or short on time. Riskier to execute
  but highest potential impact.

  2. The Core Finding: [your most surprising result, fully displayed]
  3. Understanding the Attacks (taxonomy, justified by Section 2)
  4. Stress-Testing the Finding (modifications, scenarios)
  5. Interrogating the Finding (memorization, MIA, disparate impact)
  6. Implications

  ---
  Specific Recommendations

  - Drop 2.5 and 2.6 from the taxonomy; integrate 2.6 into the intro's "related work" paragraph.
  - Put one concrete result table in Section 2 — even a partial one (e.g., adult dataset only). Give the reader something to hold.
  - Ensembling: pick two things to show — the accuracy-vs-size curve and one heatmap. The four-method ensemble-finding exercise belongs in the appendix.
  - Section 3's claim should be about DP, not "data types." DP epsilon is the central policy lever. Structure 3 around that, with data types as evidence.
  - Threat model should appear in the intro, not buried in 1.3 as an afterthought.

- CTGAN/ARF/Synthpop cluster around 23–30%; they preserve marginals but not
  high-order dependencies as faithfully.

3.3  The choice of quasi-identifiers
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DATA: QI analysis sweep (run_qi_sweep.py, April 2026). All attacks × all SDG
methods on adult (10k) and CDC Diabetes (1k), QI_tiny/QI1/QI_large/QI_behavioral.
Metric: RA averaged over attacks and SDGs.

ADULT (RF, averaged over all SDGs):
  QI_tiny    (|QI|= 3, age/sex/race only):               RF=12.6%, NB=13.6%
  QI1        (|QI|= 6, + education/natl-country/marital): RF=24.9%, KNN=22.8%
  QI_large   (|QI|=10, + occupation/workclass/etc.):      RF=28.7%, MLP=26.6%
  QI_behavioral (|QI|=6, employment features only):       RF=33.0%, MLP=32.7%

KEY FINDINGS (adult):
1. RA-vs-QI-size curve saturates: tiny→QI1 gains +12pp; QI1→large gains only
   +4pp. Doubling the QI size from 6→10 adds relatively little.
2. COMPOSITION DOMINATES SIZE: QI_behavioral (|QI|=6, employment prior) gives
   RF=33.0% — substantially higher than QI_large (|QI|=10, demographic+employment)
   at 28.7%. Knowing occupation/workclass/relationship is more informative for
   predicting income/education-num/capital than knowing age/sex/race/education.
   This is the key story: adversary knowledge quality matters more than quantity.
3. Mode/Random baseline rises with QI size (9.7% → 10.4% → 11.5%) — the
   marginal baseline also improves, but more slowly than ML attacks.

CDC DIABETES (RF, averaged over all SDGs):
  QI_tiny    (|QI|= 4, Sex/Age/BMI/GenHlth):             RF=46.9%
  QI1        (|QI|=10, + HighBP/HighChol/Smoker/etc.):   RF=49.1%
  QI_large   (|QI|=16, adds 6 lifestyle features to QI): RF=41.1%  (!)
  QI_behavioral (|QI|=10, lifestyle/behavior features):   RF=30.6%  (!)

KEY FINDINGS (CDC):
1. More QI can lower average RA: QI_large adds easy features (DiffWalk,
   CholCheck) to QI, removing them from the hidden set. Average RA drops because
   only harder features remain hidden. This illustrates that RA is a property of
   the hidden SET, not just QI size.
2. BEHAVIORAL vs. DEMOGRAPHIC FRAMING: QI_behavioral (lifestyle prior) gives
   RF=30.6% while QI1 (demographic prior) gives RF=49.1% — at the same |QI|=10.
   QI_behavioral makes demographic identity (age, sex, BMI) the hidden target,
   which is much harder to reconstruct from behavioral features than vice versa.
   This has direct policy relevance: in health datasets, demographic identity may
   be more privacy-sensitive AND harder to reconstruct from behavioral signals.
3. Mode/Random baseline is ~41–42% across all QI variants — driven by feature
   cardinality, not QI. Relative gains of ML attacks are consistent (~7–9pp).
